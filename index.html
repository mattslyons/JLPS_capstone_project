HealthCAir

Quantifying the impact of air pollution on pediatric health outcomes

Overview

Air pollution is a pervasive, global issue worsening due to climate change, increasing population, deforestation, and other causes. Among different types of air pollution, PM2.5 pollution, which consists of fine particulate matter smaller than 2.5 microns, poses significant health effects as it can enter the bloodstream.

Prior studies have found that proximity to sources of pollution is associated with increased risk of childhood cancer, cardiovascular and respiratory illnesses, end-stage renal disease, and diabetes (Brender, Maantay, and Chakraborty, 2011), but the breadth and extent of contemporaneous and long-term impacts of PM2.5 exposure on chronic health conditions among children is not well-understood.

We develop a causal model for estimating the effects of pollution exposure on pediatric health outcomes using variation in local air pollution driven by wind speed, wind direction, pollution emitted by pollution sites, and proximity of pollution sites to schools. We study these effects among the pediatric population in the state of California during the years 2000 through 2017.

Project Aims

We aim to examine the link between PM2.5 pollution exposure and adverse pediatric health outcomes throughout the state of California from 2000 to 2017.

  • Aim 1: Issues of air quality can affect all children. However, some may be disproportionately impacted by having high concentrations of PM2.5 at or near their school. To understand this issue better, the first goal is to build unique high-quality data of PM2.5 pollution exposure at every school in CA.
  • Aim 2: Establish an empirical relationship between PM2.5 levels recorded at schools in CA and being upwind from key point sources of pollution.
  • Aim 3: Estimate the causal effect of a school being located downwind from key pollution sources on selected chronic health conditions among children under the age of 19.
  • Aim 4: Conduct a counterfactual study to compare and quantify the benefits of various air pollution mitigation strategies.
  • Aim 5: Develop a model to predict future emergency room visits and hospitalization rates for hematopoietic cancers, type 1 diabetes, cardiorespiratory cancers, blood disease, blood vessel disease, all cancers, and all non-blood cancers among children 0-19 years old, using demographics, pollution properties, and time-series trends in addition to wind.

Datasets


Wind

The goal of including wind data was to properly construct an instrument that predicts the amount of PM2.5 at each school based on the location of nearby pollution point sources. We find that as the wind blows directly from a pollution source towards a school, we see higher levels of PM2.5 measurements at the school.

The wind data used in our study came from a dataset called ERA5. ERA5 contains hourly wind observations measured every 31km across the globe. Each measurement provides information such as wind speed and wind direction. We found the nearest wind measurement for every school and pollution source in our dataset and used this information to help construct our instrument


Medical Diagnoses

Pediatric medical diagnosis data come from the California Department of Public Health (CDPH) and the California Office of Statewide Health Planning and Development (OSHPD). This dataset contains HIPPA-protected medical data including demographic info about each patient and the list of diagnoses and procedures for each individual visit in the state of California from 2000 through 2017. However, for the purposes of our project, we were interested in studying the quantity of specific medical diagnoses aggregated to the month and zip code level.

Because we are not specialists in the medical field, we consulted with a medical doctor to better understand which pediatric health outcomes would be the most plausible to have a causal link with pm2.5. After our discussions and research, we decided to focus on the following pediatric medical conditions in our study:

  1. Hematopoietic cancer
  2. Type 1 diabetes
  3. All malignant cancers
  4. All non-blood malignant cancers
  5. Cardiorespiratory cancer
  6. Blood diseases
  7. Blood vessel diseases
  8. Both blood and blood vessel diseases
  9. Respiratory diseases (used as a control diagnosis for which we expect to see strong significance)
  10. Injuries (used as a control diagnosis for which we expect to see no significance)


PM2.5 Readings

PM2.5, or particulate matter 2.5, are fine droplets of pollution in the air that are 2.5 microns or less in width. PM2.5 is believed to be the most deadly due to its ability to enter the bloodstream. We collected PM2.5 data from the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and satellite imagery.

This data visualization shows the average PM2.5 levels from 2000 through 2017. Only the zip codes which had an open school during the time period of our study (2000-2017) were considered in our analysis and are displayed in the graph. We observe that PM2.5 levels are generally the highest in the Los Angeles region with average PM2.5 levels as high as 18 tons per year. Similarly, the central valley of California has also seen high levels of PM2.5 with average PM2.5 levels in the range of 12-17 tons per year.


Pollution Sources

Comes from the EPA


Schools

The California schools dataset comes from the California School Campus Database (CSCD). The list contains over 10,000 California schools ranging from kindergarten to high school. Each school contains information such as the date the school opened, its closing date if applicable, school district, address, grades offered, and whether it's a magnet school.

An assumption we've made in our study is that every child attends a school in the same zip code as their home. We've used each patient's home zip code and assume their PM2.5 exposure is equal to what was measured at the school(s) in their zip code. Then by using the PM2.5 exposure amounts at each school, we can predict the quantity of selected medical outcomes.


Population

Population data come from the US Census Bureau. The Census Bureau performs an official census every 10 years. Thus, we used these official census counts for the years 2000 and 2010. For the years 2001 through 2009, we linearly interpolated the populations for each individual zip code assuming a constant growth rate between the years 2000 through 2010. For the years 2011 through 2017, we used estimated census counts calculated by the American Community Survey (ACS). Every year, over 3.5 million US households are contacted to participate in the ACS. These survey results are then used to estimate population counts by zip code.

It was important for us to use population data in our analysis because we were interested in measuring the proportion of each zip code's population that had a given medical diagnosis. By using proportions instead of absolute counts, we are able to compare results across different zip codes more easily.

Modeling

Instrumental Variables Regression

We model each of our three health outcomes using a two-stage least squares instrumental variables (2SLS IV) regression. This modeling technique involves a two-stage process:

  1. Model PM2.5 using our instrument and fixed effects.
  2. Model the number of positive medical diagnoses using this predicted pm2.5 and same fixed effects.
A hospital visit in a given month for our selected medical conditions is not just a result of the PM2.5 levels in the same month, but rather a cumulation of PM2.5 exposure over several months. For this reason, we've decided to use a look back at the prior three-month rolling average PM2.5 value for a given month to predict the proceeding nine-month rolling average count of medical outcomes.

This process can be represented by the following notation:

\[ \widehat{PM}_{2.5}=\beta_0 + \beta_1 I + \beta_2 year + \beta_3 month + \beta_4 county + \epsilon \]

\[ Y = \frac{\sum Diagnoses}{Pop_{zipcode, 0-19}} = \delta_0 + \delta_1 \widehat{PM}_{2.5} + \delta_2 year + \delta_3 month + \delta_4 county + \epsilon \]

  • \( \widehat{PM}_{2.5} \) = the predicted PM2.5 three month average for every zip code and month
  • \( \beta_i \) = the first stage regression coefficient for the i'th variable
  • \( \delta_i \) = the second stage regression coefficient for the i'th variable.

The instrument, \(I\), is calculated using several different variables involving wind, pollution, and distance variables. We've considered the nearest 20 pollution point sources to each school, and for each, we've used their tons of PM2.5 emitted each year (TPY), distance to the school, wind speed, and wind direction to compute this instrument.

\[I = \sum_{ps=1}^{3} \sum_{d_m=1}^{D_m} \theta_{zd_m} * TPY_{ps} * \frac{S_{zd_m}}{D_{ps}} \]

  • \( z \) = zip code
  • \( m \) = month
  • \( ps \) = pollution point source
  • \( D_m \) = number of days in month
  • \( d_m \) = day of month
  • \( TPY \) = point source pollution output (tons per year)
  • \( S_{zd_m} \) = wind speed
  • \( D_{ps} \) = distance to pollution point source

We've applied this regression framework to each of our ten medical outcomes of interest. Our second-stage regression model predicts the number of positive medical diagnoses per 1,000 people using the pediatric population.


Counterfactual Analysis

After determining which medical outcomes are most related to PM2.5 through the instrumental variables regression framework, we conducted a counterfactual analysis to see how the predicted number of medical diagnoses would differ under various levels of PM2.5.
